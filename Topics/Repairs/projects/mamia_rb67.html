Mamiya RB67 Shutter Release

This was a fun repair as I was able to apply my knowledge of material engineering to complete it.

The issue this time was with a Mamiya RB67 film camera that my sister had bought. It was in great condition except for the shutter release port which can be seen in left picture as the silver button to the bottom left of the lens on the camera body. The shutter release port is so you can insert the shutter release cable and trigger the camera's shutter without touching the body of the camera itself.

As for why, don't ask me I'm not a photographer.

Regardless the port had part of a previous shutter release cable broken off stuck inside the threads. To make a bad situation worse the broken off piece of the previous cable was misaligned with the threads. In such a tight space the only tools that were small enough to fit could not handle the amount of force needed to remove the broken piece and I could not be sure that that force would not damage the cable port as well.

Now we get into the interesting part. The camera is quite nice and uses a very specific metal to prevent corrosion. Chromium. The cheap shutter release cable jack was made out of common brass. Chromium has a thermal expansion coefficient of about 4.9×10-6 K-1 while brass has a thermal expansion coefficient of about 19×10-6 K-1. That means that brass expands and shrinks almost four times the amount chromium does over the same change in temperature.

With this in mind I retrieved an ice cube wrapped it in some tissues and lowered the temperature of the shutter release button. The chromium and brass cooled and the broken brass piece became loose enough that I was able to simply reach in with fine tweezers and coax it out with no large amount of force required. As such my reward for saving my sister an expensive specialty camera repair was a single portrait picture taken with the now functioning camera using the last piece of film.

This project is in stage 6
